2020, Remote
Streamlining the parking experience + optimizing for scalability
JAPA, or Just A Parking App, is a free mobile guidance app for consumers that provides access to accurate real-time parking availability, parking lot information, and policies. The free app allows users to view spots ahead of time to plan out their parking experience before leaving the house.
Duration
6 weeks
Tools
Figma, Notion, Google Sheets
Team
Myself, Briana Omori, Kiara Cowderoy, Edward Chew
My Role
UI/UX Designer, Parking Layout, Driver Safety
The Story
My team and I were challenged to build off of the current JAPA app and create an improved, new design.

The main objectives:

1) Redesign the entire app to provide users with a smooth and pleasant experience
2) Design for scalability and future feature integrations

My Role:

I designed and prototyped the parking layout and driver safety aspect of our product as well as conducted usability and A/B testing to validate the final forms. My team and I all contributed to research, idea generation, and the final presentation. We all worked frequently and closely with JAPA's CTO to get feedback and build our solutions.
Problem Space
What is the current parking experience like and why is it a problem?
Before the COVID-19 spread, people had places to be and time was of the essence. Just when they thought they had enough problems, looking for parking took its place on the neverending list. Simply put, parking is challenging. Lots and garages are often very crowded, regulations and pricing can’t be determined until arrival, and the time spent looking for parking is unpredictable. Because of these reasons, drivers are forced to leave their house earlier to account for time spent searching for a space and bounce between parking facilities that are full. Drivers are left frustrated and now they’re late for their commitments.
Current Experience
So how is JAPA tackling this problem?
This is what the current app experience looks like. In this quick demo, the user is looking for the Southwest Parking Lot and checking the real-time space availability.
Problem
Given the platform is available in many frequented and heavily populated locations, why aren’t users engaging with the app?
While JAPA fills the need for a real-time guidance platform that provides users with parking options and reliable, live information and updates, the app is more focused on simple functionality than catering toward a variety of needs and fostering an emotional or personal connection with users.
Space for opportunity
What if we designed around the user’s personal narrative and real parking experience to guide them through using the app?
The Solution
We designed a set of new features within the JAPA app that supports a much wider range of needs. The new design takes new and returning users on a more controlled, personalized journey and uses unique features such as the map quick look to list view, filtering, and garage level card swipe to guide drivers smoothly to their destination. By integrating these features and mimicking the in-app journey to the external parking experience, JAPA creates a more full, guided experience for users and builds a more lasting relationship with them.
Let's break it down.
part 1
Directed Onboarding
After opening the app, new users are prompted to create an account by entering their phone number, name, vehicle information, and preferred payment method. This serves to make all future parking experiences simpler, such as paying for parking and identifying where they last parked.

Once finished or skipped, users can choose to learn how to use the app in 6 simple onboarding steps.
Payment Settings
Entering their payment information allows for future integration of an in-app parking ticket payment system. Users can easily make purchases and keep track of their payment history.
part 2
Location Search and Filter
Instead of first choosing from a list of all parking lots and garages compatible with JAPA, users can freely search for their destination. This new workflow and structure allows for easy scalability and gives more control to users.

To further narrow the search, users can customize their preferences with the filter feature.
Quick Look to List View
Quick look serves to show users parking availability without having to click into a lot or garage for more information. List view serves to include users that may be short on time and want to see all the nearby lots at once.
Parking Facility Information
The parking facility information page has been broken down into more digestible parts, optimized for quick eye scanning. Users can see pricing, hours, parking availability, directions, contact information, travel time, and any key information.
Parking Layout
Once users have committed to a parking facility, the ability to check multiple levels is available. In the new parking layout, users can easily decide which level to park on based on the color coded parking spots. If a level is predominantly red, users can rule it out immediately.
Driver Safety
Alerts users that they cannot use their phone while driving and all functions will be disabled until they've reached a stop. This will ensure that drivers avoid unsafe driving unless there is a passenger in the vehicle.
part 3
Saved Lots
Users can save their frequented lots/garages to check parking availability quickly without having to search for the location. They can also delete any locations they no longer visit.
How did we get there?
Behind the Scenes
Exploration and Saturation
To understand our problem space, we immersed ourselves in JAPA's business goals, their current app experience, and the market landscape.
Business to Human Questions
We translated company-centric goals and habits into customer-centric questions that would guide us to better empathize with and understand current and prospective customers.

This exercise helped us put ourselves in the users' shoes, while still considering JAPA as a business and its goals for operational and financial outcomes.
How are users currently interacting with the product?
User task analysis was used to eliminate any biases or assumptions we held prior as well as brainstorm ways to lessen the users' mental workload and eliminate any unnecessary steps or screens. We had 8 tasks for users to accomplish and observed and recorded their actions.

This user task analysis revealed that the UI wasn't indicative of function, meaning that users experienced unexpected results from the actions they took, and all around, users wanted more guidance throughout the app.
What's the current market landscape like?
To determine JAPA's current strengths and weaknesses, we analyzed their competitors in the market. I proposed we synthesize our data into a matrix to see any patterns or areas of weakness more easily.

Through this evaluation, we were able to establish what made JAPA unique, what areas they're lacking in, and what features and strategies were working for their competition. We were also able to get a better understanding of the market landscape and determine where JAPA stands in it.

The Fun Part: Iterate, Usability Tests, Repeat
For each of our focus areas of the app, we all conducted usability testing and continued iterating until reaching the product's final forms.

I'll be focusing on what I did in this section. I built off the original app's bare bones structure and designed an interactive parking facility layout. After I went through several iterations of wireframes and mid-fidelity prototypes and incorporated feedback from the team, I prepared some high-fidelity prototypes for A/B and usability testing.
Iteration 01
Takeaways

1. Too much effort
Users expressed that it would be difficult to see each level especially while driving because they would have to press the "Change Level" button and choose a level each time.

2. Unclear color conventions
Users expressed that the grey colored spots didn't indicate "unavailable" to them but rather "out of order."
Iteration 02
Takeaways

1. Difficult to see entire level
Users expressed that seeing the entire level would be difficult to do while driving because they would have to move the layout around to see it in its entirety.

2. Unclear level change function
I noticed that users were confused trying to change levels as they tried to swipe on the numbers instead of click on them.
What was most easily understood by our users?
A/B Testing Takeaways

1. Brighter red
We found that the red parking spot icon on the right was best because users expressed it was more vibrant, bold, and more quickly identified as an occupied space.

2. Star symbol
We found that users thought the initial R symbol meant reserved not restricted, so we swapped it out for a star because it’s more universally known and indicated some kind of special status. In addition, we added an explanation of what the star meant to clarify its meaning even further.
Iteration 03
Takeaways

1. Unclear buttons
All users thought that the "Level 1" title was a button and tried to click it. They expressed that it looked like a button because we consistently used this pill shape throughout the rest of the app.

2. No zooming in
I noticed that most users didn't try to click on the parking layout cards (photo on the left) because there was nothing to indicate that it was clickable.
So what did the final solution look like?
Improvements

In the final design, I took the users' feedback and turned the "Level 1" title into a button that allowed users to hop in between levels more quickly, not having to swipe through each one. I did this to account for garages that may have 10 or more levels and would be too much effort to swipe through.

In addition, I added a magnifying glass in the bottom right corner of each card to indicate that they could zoom in to see the level more closely.
Reflection
Many firsts!
Working on a cross-functional team
This was my first time working with other designers as well as the CTO! It was really exciting for me because, I'll be honest, collaborating with people I don't already know is a challenge for me. It takes some time for me to get comfortable and be vulnerable sharing ideas and giving critiques. Even though it was challenging at first, it was so rewarding for me. I loved seeing our team dynamic evolve as time went on and getting more comfortable with each other. Working closely with JAPA's CTO was also a new and exciting experience! I've never had to present my work for non-designers and throughout this process, I learned how to present my designs in a way that was understandable to someone who wasn't directly involved in the process and support my design decisions with evidence.
Remote collaboration
Just like everyone else, this was my first time collaborating remotely. There's definitely something about physically working around other people that I miss, but this constraint offered a unique opportunity to learn how to work efficiently and effectively without the proper resources/materials. I learned how to conduct usability testing and interviews through Zoom (which is actually not as terrible as you'd think). I realized how important it is to document everything you're doing and explain concepts clearly to the users you're working with.
Advocating for the end-user
Like I mentioned, usability testing is the fun part and honestly, it really was. I loved iterating on the feedback users gave and I learned how important it is to always advocate for the end-user. It was humbling because I was reminded that my assumptions are definitely not always right and I'm not designing for myself, but for others.
